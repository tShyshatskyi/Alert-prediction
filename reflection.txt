What went wrong.
Most early failures were environment and data traps, not modeling. Files looked "corrupted"
(a PyCharm large-file display limit, then a removed pandas API, offset.delta); AI-generated code
shipped a silent synthetic-data fallback and a persistence baseline that was worse than a trivial
constant. Two data issues mattered most: Nikopol's dramatic "rising trend" was partly an artifact
of the alerting granularity changing over time, and each region's series ended at that region's
last alert, which shifted forecasts into the past for quiet regions.

How I adjusted.
I stopped trusting outputs at face value and verified every step independently — recomputing
metrics, inspecting raw bytes, and benchmarking against trivial constants. I then fixed the pandas
call, removed the synthetic fallback, corrected the Nikopol filter to include oblast-level alerts,
and anchored every series to a common "now."

Why the final version is better.
It is leakage-free (rolling, time-based backtest), built on corrected data, and benchmarked against
honest baselines — so its conclusions (frontline regions predictable, Kyiv largely not, no single
method dominating) are trustworthy rather than flattering. The clearest next step: predict actual
shelling, not just the precautionary alert, by fusing alerts with intelligence, OSINT and media.
