What went wrong.
My first attempts kept hitting environment and data traps rather than modeling
problems. Files looked "corrupted" (in fact PyCharm's large-file display limit,
and separately a removed pandas API, offset.delta). AI-generated code shipped a
silent synthetic-data fallback that could have faked results, and a persistence
baseline that turned out worse than a trivial constant. Most importantly,
Nikopol's dramatic "rising trend" was partly an artifact: early oblast-level
alerts were being filtered out.

How I adjusted.
I stopped trusting outputs at face value and verified every step independently —
recomputing metrics, inspecting raw bytes, and benchmarking baselines against
trivial constants. I fixed the pandas call, removed the synthetic fallback,
added honest baselines, and corrected the Nikopol filter to include oblast-level
alerts. I also wrongly blamed Python 3.14 at one point and corrected myself once
the real cause (a library API change) was found.

Why the final version is better.
It is leakage-free (rolling, time-based backtest), grounded in corrected data,
and benchmarked against honest baselines — so its conclusions (Nikopol
predictable, Kyiv largely not, no single method dominating) are trustworthy
rather than flattering.

Limitation / what I would do next.
A binary "alert" is a coarse, precautionary signal — not an actual strike.
Predicting real shelling would be far more meaningful and far harder: it would
mean fusing alerts with other correlating signals (intelligence reports, OSINT,
media statements, launch/telemetry data) rather than the siren feed alone.
